The two pricing models

Almost every move falls into one of two pricing models.

Hourly. Used for local moves. You’re billed for crew time, from arrival at pickup to last item placed at the destination. Standard for moves under about 50 miles.

Binding (sometimes called flat-rate). Used for long-distance moves. The mover sends a fixed price upfront based on weight, distance, and truck space. The number doesn’t change between quote and delivery.

There’s also “non-binding estimate” — a number that can change at delivery. We don’t use it. Most reputable long-distance movers don’t either.

How hourly pricing actually works

Three numbers go into every hourly quote:

1. The hourly rate. Based on crew size. Two movers is one rate. Three movers is a higher rate. Four movers is higher still. The rate is for the full crew, not per mover.

2. The minimum. Most movers set a minimum number of hours. Two-hour minimum for a small crew is standard. Below that, the move isn’t worth dispatching.

3. The travel fee. Covers drive time from the mover’s warehouse to your pickup, and from your destination back. Some movers bill it as a flat charge. Some hide it inside the hourly rate. Some bill drive time both ways at the full hourly rate, which costs more.

Sparkle’s travel fee is one flat charge equal to one hour of crew time. Billed once, not each way.

For our exact rates, minimums, and travel fee, see the Pricing page →.

How binding quotes actually work

For long-distance moves, the mover takes an inventory of what you’re shipping (in person or by video), estimates the weight, and locks in a price.

A real binding quote includes:

  • Total cost
  • Weight allowance (the inventory you described)
  • Pickup date
  • Delivery window (a real range, not “two to three weeks”)
  • Which truck the load is on (dedicated or shared)

If anything in that list is missing, it’s not a binding quote. It’s a non-binding estimate dressed up.

Where the surprises usually come from

Three places, mostly:

1. Fees that aren’t in the quote. Fuel surcharge, stairs charge, long-carry charge, “heavy item” fee for standard furniture, weekend premium, after-hours premium. Each one $50–$300. They add up.

2. Hours that should be free but aren’t. Drive time billed both ways at full rate. Disassembly and reassembly billed separately. Pad and shrink-wrap charged per item.

3. The non-binding shuffle. The mover quotes one weight at booking. At the warehouse, the actual weight comes in higher. The price goes up at delivery. You pay the new number to get your stuff.

None of these are illegal. Most movers disclose them somewhere in the contract. The cost is real, just not visible at quote time.

What we do differently

We send a binding quote when we can. Long-distance moves get a number locked in: cost, weight allowance, delivery window, dedicated truck. Doesn’t move between booking and delivery.

What to ask any mover before booking

Whether you book with us or not, ask these:

  1. Is the quote binding or non-binding? If non-binding, ask what would trigger a price change at delivery.
  2. What fees aren’t in the hourly rate? Ask specifically about fuel surcharges, weekend premiums, stair fees, long-carry fees, and “heavy item” charges for standard furniture.
  3. How is the travel fee calculated? Flat hour, billed both ways, or rolled into the hourly rate?
  4. Is the truck dedicated or shared? For long-distance, this is the difference between a 3-day delivery and a 3-week one.
  5. Is the crew on payroll or contracted out? Subcontracted crews have less accountability if something goes wrong.
  6. What’s the deposit? Anything over $0–$200 for a residential move is on the high side. We don’t take any.

If a mover hesitates on any of these, that’s the answer.

FAQ

Why is hourly pricing standard for local moves?

Local moves vary too much in time and complexity for a flat rate to be fair to either side. Stairs, parking, building access, and how packed the home is all change the time required. Hourly billing keeps the price honest.

Why is binding pricing standard for long-distance?

Long-distance moves have predictable costs once weight and distance are known. The variables are smaller, so a fixed number works. Hourly would be impossible to estimate across multi-day transit.

What’s the average cost of a local move in Chicago?

2-bedroom apartment: $800–$1,500 typical range. Studios run $400–$900. 3-bedroom houses run $1,500–$3,000+. Real ranges on the Pricing page →.

What’s the average cost of a long-distance move from Chicago?

Chicago to Florida starts at $3,800. Chicago to Texas starts at $4,200. Coast-to-coast runs higher. See the Pricing page → or Long Distance Moving →.

Do most movers really hide their prices?

Most do. Industry standard is “contact us for a quote” with no rates visible on the site. The reasons are usually competitive (they don’t want to look more expensive) or operational (they don’t want to commit to a number on the web). Both are tradeoffs the customer pays for.

Is the cheapest mover usually the most expensive?

Often, yes. The lowball quote tends to come with a non-binding estimate, hidden fees, or a subcontracted crew. The final number arrives at delivery.
